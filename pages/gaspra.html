Gaspra, seen by Galileo

951 Gaspra is an asteroid from the inner asteroid belt, part of the Flora family. It was discovered in 1916 and later became the first asteroid ever to be visited by a spacecraft, when the Galileo spacecraft approached it on its way to Jupiter in 1991. Gaspra has a composition rich in olivine, possibly because of the partial differentiation of the parent body, and a less cratered surface.

Discovey

Gaspra was discovered by Russian astronomer Grigory N. Neujmin at the Simiez Observatory in Crimea in 1916.

Name

Neujmin named the asteroid after Gaspra, a Black Sea retreat and a spa town in Crimea.

Orbit and rotation

Gaspra is located in the inner asteroid belt at 2.21 AU from the Sun, with a 1.83 AU perihelion and a 2.59 AU aphelion. The eccentricity and inclination are respectively 0.17 and 4.11° The orbital period is 3.29 years and the rotational period is 7.04 hours.

Gaspra is part of the Flora collisional family, named after its largest member, and includes more than 13 000 known members.

Physical characteristics

Shape and size

Gaspra has triaxial measurements of 18.2 km × 10.5 km × 8.9 km and a mean diameter of 12.2 km. It has an elongated and angular shape.

Structure

Gaspra is an S-type asteroid. Compared to other such asteroids, Gaspra is richer in olivine. This composition suggests that the parent body of the Flora family was partially differentiated. The mass and the density haven't been determined during the Galileo visit.

Gaspra does not have large craters, likely because the Flora collision was relatively recent - 200 million years ago. The largest named crater (Saratoga) measures only 2.8 km in diameter. For that reason relatively flat regions like Dunne Regio remain. Apart from small impact craters, several grooves up to 2.5 km in length mark the surface.

Exploration

Gaspra was the first asteroid to be visited by a spacecraft in 1991 by the Galileo mission, while on its way to Jupiter. Initially, Gaspra's position was only known to within about 200 km and the camera's field of view was only about 5° across. This posed a challenge, as the encounter would bear no results. This lead to the implementation of a pioneering robot localization campaign. This was successful and allowed the spacecraft to obtain images from as close as 5,300 km. After this flyby, Galileo visited one more asteroid - 243 Ida.
